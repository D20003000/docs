🧪 Cglab 920 Spectroscopy Experiment

Quantitative Exploration of Carbon Quantum Dots Using Cglab Fluorescence Spectrometer

A laboratory protocol to validate the linear relationship between fluorescence intensity and concentration.

I. Objective

This experiment aims to use the Cglab Fluorescence Spectrometer to prepare carbon quantum dot solutions at various concentrations, verify the linear relationship between fluorescence intensity and concentration, and explore the applicable boundaries of the Beer-Lambert Law in fluorescence analysis.

II. Principles

1. Synthesis and Fluorescence of Carbon Quantum Dots
Sugars undergo dehydration and carbonization at high temperatures, forming nanoscale carbon particles (carbon quantum dots) with rich oxygen-containing functional groups (e.g., hydroxyl, carboxyl). When excited by 390 nm UV light, these dots emit stable blue-green fluorescence. The fluorescence intensity is directly proportional to the number of emitting particles in the solution.

2. Beer-Lambert Law in Fluorescence Analysis
In dilute solutions, the fluorescence intensity (F) is proportional to the concentration (c) of the fluorescent substance, which can be expressed as:

F = K · c

However, at high concentrations, the inner filter effect (where the solution absorbs the excitation or emitted light) will cause the intensity to deviate from linearity or even decrease.

III. Materials and Instruments
  • Materials: White granulated sugar, distilled water, quartz cuvettes (square), graduated cylinders, beakers, glass rods, filter paper.
  • Main Instruments: Cglab Fluorescence Spectrometer (with 390 nm UV excitation source), electronic balance, heating equipment.
IV. Procedure

1. Preparation of Stock Solution
Dissolve 1.0 g of caramelized sugar in 100 mL distilled water, stir for 10 minutes to aid dissolution. Cool and filter. The filtrate is the stock solution (marked as concentration C₀), appearing light amber.

V. Data Processing and Analysis

Plot a scatter graph with Relative Concentration (c) on the x-axis and Fluorescence Intensity (F) on the y-axis, and perform a linear fit.

  • Verification of Linearity: In the low-concentration region (typically the first 4 points), if R² > 0.97, the relationship is considered linear, confirming the Beer-Lambert Law.
  • Regression Equation: Calculate the linear regression equation F = k·c + b. The slope k can be interpreted as the relative apparent quantum yield of the carbon quantum dots.
  • Deviation Observation: Observe whether the 5th point (the stock solution) deviates from the fitted line. If its intensity is lower than predicted or even lower than a mid-concentration sample, it indicates the inner filter effect.
VI. Expected Results and Discussion
  • In the low-concentration range (c = 0 ~ 0.75), fluorescence intensity increases linearly with concentration, validating the Beer-Lambert Law for dilute solutions.
  • At high concentrations (c = 1.00), if the fluorescence intensity growth slows or decreases, it demonstrates the dominance of the inner filter effect, revealing the physical limit of concentration in fluorescence analysis.
  • This experiment provides an intuitive understanding of the conditions and scope of the linear relationship between fluorescence and concentration, establishing a methodological foundation for future quantitative analysis.
